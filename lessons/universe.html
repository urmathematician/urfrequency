    Stars

    Definition

    A star is not just a hot ball of burning gases, but rather a ball of gases undergoing nuclear fusion inside it. The Sun, for example, makes up 99% of the mass in our solar system. Stars are held together by their own gravity, and they play a crucial role in shaping galaxies and the universe as a whole.

    Birth of Stars

    Stars are born from massive clouds of gas and dust in space, known as molecular clouds.

    Gravitational forces cause these clouds to contract into smaller regions, forming protostars.

    You might wonder, if stars are born from gas clouds, then how does gravity arise in the first place? The answer lies in dark matter. Dark matter, which has mass, helps generate gravitational forces. This gravitational force pulls gases together, and through the strong gravity and the collision of atomic nuclei, nuclear fusion begins. This process marks the birth of a star.

    Planets

    Planets are celestial bodies that have a particular shape and size, often accompanied by moons. They form in the middle age of stars and are born when a star releases excess energy or when a large object impacts the star.

    Death of Stars

    Low Mass Stars

    Stars like the Sun will eventually exhaust their hydrogen fuel in the core. As hydrogen fusion decreases, the core contracts, and the outer layers expand, forming a red giant. In the end, these stars shed their outer layers, creating a planetary nebula, and the core becomes a white dwarf. This white dwarf will never undergo nuclear fusion again. It is theorized that, after a long time, the white dwarf could eventually become a black dwarf.

    High Mass Stars

    Massive stars have shorter lifespans. They undergo a more complex series of fusion reactions, producing heavier elements like carbon, oxygen, and eventually iron. Since iron cannot undergo fusion to release energy, the core collapses, triggering a catastrophic event known as a supernova. The remnants of the core can become a neutron star or, in the most massive cases, a black hole.

    Types of Stars

    Main Sequence Stars

    Main Sequence stars, including the Sun, have hydrogen as their fuel and are in the primal period of their life. They are in the stable phase of fusion, where hydrogen is converted into helium in their cores.

    Red Giants

    Red Giants are in their last stage. They have burned through all their fuel and increased their mass to gain more fuel. An example of a red giant is UY Scuti.

    White Dwarfs

    White dwarfs were once like the Sun, but when their fuel runs out, they collapse due to their own gravity. They no longer undergo nuclear fusion and gradually cool over time. The end stage of a white dwarf is theorized to be a black dwarf.

    Blue Giants

    Blue Giants are massive, extremely hot, and incredibly bright (luminous stars). They are also known as young stars. When their appearance is blue mixed with white, it means they are so young that molecular clouds are visible around them. Stars like Alnitak, Alnilam, Mintaka (from the Orion belt), and Sirius are examples of blue giants. When they die, they can become a supernova, a neutron star, or a black hole.

    Supergiants

    Supergiants are the largest and most massive stars in the universe. They can be hundreds of times the size of the Sun. UY Scuti is the largest known star in 2024 and is colder than the Sun.

    Neutron Stars

    Neutron stars are the dense remnants of massive stars that form after a supernova. They are composed entirely of neutrons, and their diameter is only a few kilometers. When they rotate and emit radiation, they are referred to as pulsars.

    Variable Stars

    Some stars vary in brightness over time. These are called variable stars. An example of a variable star is UY Scuti.

    Protostars

    Protostars are the process of young stars forming. They have a gas-like disk surrounding them, similar to stellar nurseries found in nebulae.

    Nebula

    A nebula is a beautiful object made up of fog, mist, and ionized gases. Much like how humans leave behind remains after death, stars also leave behind their remains when they die. This remains, when dust collapses under the pull of gravity, forms stars. This dust is called a nebula.

    Nebulae as Cosmic Clouds

    Nebulae are vast cosmic clouds, spanning thousands of light years. They are composed of dust and gases that can eventually form new stars.

    Formation of Stars

    Stars are born within nebulae. Before stars are formed, they exist in nebula form. When the dust within the nebula collapses due to gravity, stars are created. Essentially, one star's remains contribute to the birth of a new star, completing a cosmic cycle.

    A white dwarf is the dead remnant of a star, and its size is no larger than that of Earth. It marks the end of a low-mass star's life cycle. When a star undergoes continuous nuclear fusion, the remnants of this process eventually form a white dwarf. These stars are extremely hot, reaching temperatures of up to 100,000¬∞C.

    Formation and Characteristics

    The gravity of a white dwarf pulls its electrons inward, causing it to collapse. According to the Pauli Exclusion Principle, no two electrons can occupy the same quantum state. This creates resistance against further compression, allowing the white dwarf to remain stable despite its intense gravitational pull.

    Long Lifespan and Binary Systems

    White dwarfs have an incredibly long lifespan, potentially lasting as long as the age of the Universe. They can also exist in binary systems, where they may interact with other stars.

    Neutron Star

    A neutron star is one of the densest objects in the universe, second only to black holes. It forms after a supernova explosion and is made entirely of neutrons. The diameter of a neutron star is only a few kilometers, and it rotates, emitting radiation. These rotating neutron stars are called pulsars.

    1. Density

    Neutron stars are incredibly dense, but not as dense as black holes. A neutron star with a radius of just 10 km has a mass equal to that of the Sun. In fact, a teaspoon of neutron star material would weigh as much as the entire Earth.

    2. Composition

    Neutron stars are primarily composed of neutrons. In their cores, protons and other particles are present, but they are so densely packed that positive and negative particles combine to form neutral neutrons.

    3. Strong Magnetic Fields

    Neutron stars possess extremely strong magnetic fields, which are billions of times stronger than Earth's magnetic field.

    4. Rapid Rotation

    Neutron stars spin rapidly, sometimes rotating up to 100 times per second. This rapid rotation is a result of the core's spin after the star's collapse during the supernova event.

    5. Gravitational Time Dilation

    Due to the extreme density of neutron stars, their gravitational pull is incredibly strong. This leads to significant time dilation, meaning time passes much slower near the surface of a neutron star.

    6. High Temperatures

    Initially, neutron stars are very hot, but as they age, they gradually cool down.

    7. Pulsars

    Pulsars are a specific type of neutron star that spins even faster. They emit regular bursts of radiation as they rotate, making them observable as "pulses" of light from Earth.

    Supernova

    A supernova is an astronomical event that occurs when a massive star reaches the end of its life and undergoes a violent expulsion of its outer layers. It is one of the most energetic and luminous phenomena in the universe. Supernovae can briefly outshine an entire galaxy, emitting more energy than the Sun will in its entire lifetime.

    1. Cause of a Supernova

    A supernova occurs when a massive star (usually several times the mass of our Sun) has exhausted its nuclear fuel. The core contracts, and the outer layers collapse inward, triggering a powerful explosion that sends the star‚Äôs material scattering through space.

    2. Types of Supernovae

    There are two main types of supernovae: Type I and Type II. Type I occurs when a white dwarf in a binary system accumulates enough material from its companion star to initiate fusion and explode. Type II happens when a massive star reaches the end of its life and its core collapses under gravity.

    3. Energy Release

    During a supernova, an immense amount of energy is released in the form of light and heat, making the event visible across vast distances. Supernovae can briefly outshine the entire galaxy in which they reside.

    4. Stellar Remnants

    After a supernova, the remains of the star can form a neutron star or a black hole, depending on the mass of the star. The expelled material enriches the surrounding space with heavier elements, contributing to the formation of new stars, planets, and other cosmic bodies.

    Molecular Cloud

    A molecular cloud, also known as a stellar nursery, is a cold and dense region in space where stars are born. These clouds are primarily composed of molecular hydrogen (H2) but also contain other molecules such as carbon monoxide (CO), ammonia (NH3), water (H2O), and various organic compounds.

    1. Temperature and Density

    The maximum temperature in molecular clouds is between 10K and 20K. They are the coldest regions in space, which helps in the formation of molecular hydrogen. These clouds are incredibly dense and closely packed, which leads to strong gravitational forces.

    2. Gravitational Collapse

    Due to the intense gravity within the molecular clouds, the material inside them begins to collapse under its own weight. As this process continues, the temperature of the material increases and the cloud starts to glow.

    3. Star Formation

    The rising temperature and pressure eventually lead to the onset of nuclear fusion, marking the birth of a new star. These stellar nurseries are the primary locations for star formation in the universe.
